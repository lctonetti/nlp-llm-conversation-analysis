To keep a table of this caliber engaged, you need questions that are intellectually chewy but tonally light. These "Hot Potato" questions are designed to bounce around the table, forcing a Nobel Prize winner to debate a country music legend on equal footing.
1. The "Accidental Genius" Prompt
"What is the most successful or 'brilliant' thing you’ve ever done that was actually a complete, embarrassing mistake?"
Why it works: This is the ultimate equalizer. High-achievers like Sara Blakely (who started Spanx by cutting the feet off pantyhose) and Richard Feynman (who won a Nobel Prize because he started calculating the physics of a wobbling cafeteria plate) will love this. It moves the conversation away from "look how great I am" to "look how lucky/clumsy I am," which builds instant rapport.
2. The "Interspecies Governance" Prompt
"If we had to replace the entire world's political leadership with a single species of animal, which species would lead us into a utopia, and which would lead us into immediate, hilarious chaos?"
Why it works: This perfectly bridges the gap between your guests. Jane Goodall can provide actual primate data; Ann Richards and Trevor Noah can lampoon current political structures; and Ben Franklin—who famously wanted the turkey to be the national bird—would have a field day defending the honor of the avian world.
3. The "Preemptive Mugshot" Prompt
"If the police showed up at this front door right now and arrested everyone at this table without explanation, what would your closest friends and family assume you finally got caught doing?"
Why it works: This is the "mischief" question. It’s a playful way to let guests poke fun at their own reputations. Dolly Parton might joke about a "glitter-smuggling ring," while Steve Wozniak might admit to a high-tech prank gone wrong. It forces everyone to reveal a bit of their "inner rebel" in a way that is inherently humorous and humanizing.
Pro-Tip for the Host:
If the conversation starts to lean too heavily into one person’s expertise (e.g., Feynman gets too deep into particle physics), use Julia Child as your "reset button." Simply ask: "Julia, if Richard’s subatomic particles were an ingredient in a sauce, would they be a thickening agent or a garnish?" It’s impossible to be stuffy when you're talking about physics through the lens of gravy.

The "Accidental Genius" Round-Table
The Host: "Alright, everyone, time for a 'Hot Potato.' What is the most successful thing you’ve ever done that was actually a complete, embarrassing mistake?"
Sara Blakely: (Laughing immediately) "Oh, I’ll take this. My entire billion-dollar company started because I was too cheap to buy new clothes! I had these cream-colored pants and no 'smooth' underwear to wear beneath them. I took a pair of scissors and cut the feet off my control-top pantyhose. I spent the whole night with the legs rolling up my thighs like window shades. It was a disaster, but I looked great in the pants. Thus, Spanx was born from a pair of ruined tights and a lack of planning!"
Richard Feynman: (Leaning forward, eyes twinkling) "That’s high-level engineering, Sara! My Nobel Prize essentially started because I was bored in a Cornell cafeteria. I saw a guy throw a dinner plate in the air like a Frisbee. I noticed the plate wobbled, but the red Cornell medallion on the plate rotated faster than the wobble. I started calculating the motion just for the 'fun' of it. People thought I was doing deep, serious research. I was actually just avoidng my real work because I was burned out. That 'cafeteria wobble' eventually led me to the equations for quantum electrodynamics."
Ann Richards: (Dryly) "Richard, honey, only a man could win a Nobel Prize for watching someone throw dishes. If a woman did that, we’d just call it 'Tuesday night with the ex-husband'!"
The table erupts in laughter. Julia Child nearly spills her wine.
Julia Child: "Exactly! I once dropped a potato pancake directly onto the stove during a live broadcast. I didn't have a backup, so I just scooped it up, plopped it back in the pan, and told the audience, 'Remember, you are alone in the kitchen, nobody can see you!' People wrote me letters for years saying it was a 'brilliant' lesson in culinary confidence. Truly, I was just terrified of losing a perfectly good potato!"
Benjamin Franklin: "Mistakes are the portals of discovery, Julia. I once attempted to electrocute a turkey for a Christmas gala—I thought the electricity would make the meat uncommonly tender. Instead, I managed to shock myself into unconsciousness in front of all my guests. When I woke up, they were all applauding, thinking I was testing the limits of human conductivity. I didn't have the heart to tell them I was just a hungry man with a very poor grip on a wire."
Dolly Parton: (Giggling) "Well, Ben, at least you had a spark! I once entered a 'Dolly Parton Lookalike Contest' at a drag club in Hollywood. I went totally over the top—higher hair, bigger everything. And I lost! To a man! It was the most embarrassing 'failure' of my life, but it became my favorite story to tell. It taught me that nobody does 'Dolly' quite like a professional, including me."
Maya Angelou: (In her deep, melodic voice) "You see? We are all just beautiful accidents waiting for a place to happen."
Why this works:
The Power Dynamics: By starting with Sara Blakely and Julia Child, you've established a "no-judgment zone" where even the most successful people can admit to being messy.
The Pivot: Feynman and Franklin provide the intellectual weight, showing that even "Hard Science" is often just a series of lucky stumbles.
The Closer: Dolly Parton and Maya Angelou bring it back to the human element, ensuring the conversation feels warm rather than competitive.
